Global Climate Change: An Overview of Current Trends and Future Projections

Climate change represents one of the most significant challenges facing humanity in the 21st century. The Earth's average surface temperature has risen by approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius since the pre-industrial era, driven primarily by the accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere. Carbon dioxide concentrations have now surpassed 420 parts per million, a level not seen in over three million years of Earth's history.

The burning of fossil fuels for energy production remains the largest single contributor to greenhouse gas emissions, accounting for roughly 75 percent of global CO2 output. Industrial processes, deforestation, and agricultural practices contribute the remainder. Methane emissions from livestock, rice paddies, and leaking natural gas infrastructure are particularly concerning because methane is over 80 times more potent than CO2 over a 20-year period.

Observed impacts of climate change are already being felt across every continent and ocean. Arctic sea ice extent has declined by approximately 13 percent per decade since satellite measurements began in 1979. Glaciers in the Himalayas, the Andes, and the Alps are retreating at accelerating rates, threatening freshwater supplies for billions of people who depend on glacial meltwater for drinking water and irrigation.

Sea levels are rising at an average rate of 3.6 millimetres per year globally, with some regions experiencing significantly higher rates due to local land subsidence and ocean circulation patterns. Coastal cities including Miami, Jakarta, and Mumbai face existential threats from inundation within this century under high-emission scenarios. Coral reef ecosystems, which support approximately 25 percent of all marine species, are experiencing unprecedented bleaching events as ocean temperatures rise.

Extreme weather events are becoming more frequent and intense. Heatwaves that would previously occur once per 50 years are now occurring approximately five times more frequently. Heavy precipitation events have intensified, while drought conditions are expanding across sub-tropical regions. The 2020 Atlantic hurricane season was the most active on record, forming 30 named storms.

Addressing climate change requires a fundamental transformation of the global energy system. Renewable energy sources, particularly solar and wind power, have experienced dramatic cost reductions of over 90 percent in the past decade, making them cost-competitive with fossil fuels in most markets. Electric vehicle adoption is accelerating, with battery costs falling by 89 percent since 2010.

International agreements such as the Paris Agreement of 2015 aim to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. However, current national pledges, known as Nationally Determined Contributions, are insufficient to achieve this target. Scientists estimate that to limit warming to 1.5 degrees, global emissions must reach net zero by approximately 2050.

Carbon capture and storage technologies offer potential pathways to remove CO2 from the atmosphere. Direct air capture facilities can extract CO2 and either store it underground or convert it into useful products. Nature-based solutions, including reforestation, soil carbon sequestration, and wetland restoration, could collectively remove several gigatons of CO2 annually.

Social equity and climate justice are increasingly central to climate policy discussions. Vulnerable communities in low-income countries bear a disproportionate share of climate impacts despite contributing least to historical emissions. Adaptation measures, including climate-resilient infrastructure, improved early warning systems, and social protection programmes, are essential for protecting these populations.

The economic costs of climate inaction far exceed the costs of transitioning to a low-carbon economy. The Stern Review estimated that unmitigated climate change could reduce global GDP by 5 to 20 percent, while the investments required for decarbonisation amount to approximately 1 to 2 percent of GDP annually. Green investments also generate substantial co-benefits including improved air quality, energy security, and job creation in emerging clean energy sectors.
